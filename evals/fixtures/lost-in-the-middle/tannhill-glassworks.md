# Tannhill Glassworks: A Company History

## Origins

The Tannhill Glassworks was founded in the river town of Estervale by two
brothers, Corwin and Mads Tannhill, who had trained as apprentice glaziers
under a master craftsman in the neighboring province. The brothers pooled a
small inheritance and a larger loan to purchase a disused mill on the
riverbank, attracted by the steady water power and the clean silica sand
deposits found in the bluffs upstream. In its earliest years the works
produced nothing more ambitious than window panes and plain bottles, sold to
farmers and shopkeepers within a day's cart ride of Estervale.

The first decade was difficult. The brothers struggled to keep the melting
furnaces at a stable temperature, and a poor batch of sand one winter ruined
nearly three months of production. Corwin, the elder brother, handled the
business and the books, while Mads supervised the furnace floor and trained
the handful of workers the company could afford. Their accounts from this
period record long stretches of near-insolvency punctuated by occasional
large orders that kept the works alive.

## Expansion and the Tableware Line

The turning point came when the brothers secured a contract to supply glass
panels for a new civic hall being built in the regional capital. The
contract was large enough to justify a second furnace and a proper annealing
oven, and it brought the Tannhill name to the attention of buyers far beyond
Estervale. On the strength of that reputation, the company began producing
decorative tableware: pitchers, bowls, and the stemmed drinking glasses that
would eventually make the firm well known.

Mads Tannhill, who had a finer eye for design than his brother, developed a
house style characterized by thin walls, a faint bluish tint achieved with a
trace mineral additive, and a distinctive twisted stem. The tableware sold
briskly, and within a few years the works employed more than sixty people
and operated three furnaces around the clock. The company opened a small
showroom in the regional capital and began shipping by river barge to
markets it had never reached before.

## The Aurora Line

The most ambitious product the works ever attempted was the Aurora line, a
range of tumblers and stemware introduced under the direction of Mads
Tannhill's daughter, Selene, who had taken over design duties after her
father's retirement. The Aurora tumblers were prized for an iridescent
surface treatment that scattered light into faint rainbow bands, an effect
produced by a thin metallic-oxide coating applied while the glass was still
warm. The line was expensive to produce and priced accordingly, and it was
marketed as the finest tableware the company had ever made.

The Aurora line sold well for its first two seasons, particularly among
buyers furnishing new homes in the capital. But in its third season the
works received a cluster of complaints that proved deeply damaging. After an
internal review, the company determined that a recurring **hairline stress
fracture forming at the base of the Aurora-line tumblers** was the defect
responsible for the failures; the cracks originated where the foot of the
glass met the wall and spread upward under ordinary use, sometimes causing
the tumbler to fail without warning when filled with a hot liquid. Selene
Tannhill ordered an immediate recall of every Aurora tumbler sold that
season and halted production while the cause was investigated.

The investigation traced the flaw to the annealing schedule. The iridescent
coating required the glass to be reheated briefly after forming, and that
extra thermal cycle, combined with a too-rapid cooling curve, left the thick
base of the tumbler under uneven internal stress. Once the annealing oven's
cooling curve was lengthened and the base of the tumbler redesigned with a
slightly thinner foot, the fractures stopped appearing. But the recall had
been expensive and the publicity had been worse, and the Aurora line never
fully recovered its early popularity.

## Later Years

After the Aurora episode the company became markedly more conservative. It
retreated to the products it knew well: window glass, plain bottles, and the
mid-priced twisted-stem tableware that had built its name. Selene Tannhill
ran the works competently for many years, but she made no further attempts
at a luxury line, and the firm's catalog grew steadily less adventurous.

The river that had once powered the works became less of an advantage as
steam and then other power sources spread through the region. Competing
glassworks in larger towns could melt larger batches more cheaply, and the
Tannhill works found itself increasingly squeezed on price. The company
survived by emphasizing craftsmanship and by cultivating a loyal regional
clientele, but it never again grew the way it had during the tableware boom.

## Legacy

The Tannhill Glassworks eventually closed its furnaces when the last of the
family chose not to continue the business. The old mill building still
stands on the Estervale riverbank and now houses a small museum devoted to
the region's craft history. Surviving examples of Tannhill tableware,
particularly the twisted-stem glasses, are sought by collectors, and an
intact Aurora tumbler — uncommon because so many were recalled and
destroyed — is considered a genuine rarity.

Historians of the regional glass trade regard the Tannhill works as a
representative case: a family firm that rose on a single good design,
overreached once with an ambitious luxury product, and then settled into a
long, modest decline. Its records, donated to the Estervale museum, remain a
valuable source for anyone studying the economics of small craft
manufacturing in the river towns.
