# The Korrin Expedition

The Korrin Expedition was the largest overland survey ever mounted across the
Sable Plateau, and for nearly two decades afterward it remained the standard
against which every later traverse was measured. This note is the consolidated
account assembled by the Cartographers' Hall from the expedition's own field
ledgers, the quartermaster's roll, and the recollections of the survivors
gathered at the closing inquiry.

## Departure

The expedition mustered at the frontier depot of Hallsreach in the early
spring, when the high passes were judged barely open. Forty-seven crew members
departed Hallsreach on the morning of the muster — a deliberately round and
well-documented figure, because the depot warden counted every traveler
through the gate and stamped the roll twice. Of those forty-seven, eleven were
surveyors, nine were porters under the quartermaster, six were hunters and
foragers, four were medics, and the remainder were guides, drovers, and the
two clerks who kept the daily ledger. The expedition's commander, Marshal
Edrin Korrin, for whom the venture is named, insisted that the full count be
read aloud at the gate so that no one could later dispute who had set out.

The column left with thirty-one pack animals, four wagons, and provisions
calculated for a hundred and forty days. The wagons carried the heavy survey
instruments — the great theodolite, the chain reels, the brass levels — along
with the grain that the animals could not be expected to find on the plateau.
Korrin's stated objective was to fix the position of every spring, ford, and
defensible camp between Hallsreach and the salt flats of the far interior, and
to do it accurately enough that the map could be trusted by caravans for a
generation.

## Organization of the Column

Before the column left Hallsreach, Korrin spent eleven days at the depot
arranging the order of march, the rotation of the survey parties, and the
chain of supply. The Cartographers' Hall preserved his march order because it
became a model copied by later expeditions almost without change.

The column moved in five sections. At the head rode the lead survey party and
two guides, charged with finding the next defensible camp and the next water.
Behind them came the instrument wagons under the quartermaster, then the main
body with the pack animals and the medics, then the second survey party
working the ground already passed, and finally a small rear guard of hunters
who doubled as foragers. Korrin himself rode wherever the day's difficulty
demanded, but his standing place was with the instrument wagons, because he
held that the instruments were the expedition's true cargo and everything else
existed to deliver them.

The quartermaster, a methodical woman named Sable Vant, kept a separate roll
from the clerks' daily ledger. Her roll tracked provisions, animal condition,
and the issue of equipment, and it is from her roll that the loss of pack
animals can be told apart from the loss of crew — a distinction the inquiry
later found essential. Vant's standing instruction, issued at Hallsreach and
never relaxed, was that every gate, ford, and cairn the column passed be
logged with the count of people and animals present, so that any discrepancy
would be caught at once.

The two clerks, Anvel and Riese, divided the day between them: Anvel kept the
morning ledger and the survey transcriptions, Riese kept the afternoon ledger
and the fair copies. They were the youngest members of the expedition and the
only two who never carried a survey instrument or a hunting bow, a fact that
Korrin defended at the inquiry on the grounds that the ledgers were worth more
than another pair of hands.

## Provisioning and the Hundred-and-Forty-Day Plan

The provisioning was built on a hundred-and-forty-day plan, with the outbound
leg budgeted at sixty days, the work at the salt flats at twenty, and the
return at sixty. The grain in the wagons was meant to feed the animals for the
first half of the journey, after which the column expected to rely on plateau
forage. The hunters carried bows and a small reserve of dried meat, and the
plan assumed the basins would yield game.

In retrospect the inquiry judged this plan optimistic in two specific ways.
First, it allowed no margin for a long dry crossing, and the Bonewalk would
prove longer and drier than any lore suggested. Second, it assumed the
interior basins held game at the level the guides remembered from earlier
seasons, when in fact the foraging proved thin. Korrin's later defense was
that a column provisioned for every contingency would have been too heavy to
move, and the inquiry, while it recorded the criticism, did not dispute the
underlying logic.

## The First Leg

The first three weeks went well. The column climbed the Hallsreach stair, a
switchbacked track cut into the plateau's western rampart, and emerged onto
the open grassland without incident. The surveyors worked in two rotating
parties, one ranging ahead to scout the next camp while the other fixed the
position of the current one. The clerks recorded each day's distance, the
weather, the state of the animals, and any landmark worth a name. By the end
of the first month the expedition had charted the western springs and given
formal names to the Twin Cisterns, the Whistling Gap, and the long dry wash
the guides called the Bonewalk.

It was on the Bonewalk that the first real difficulty appeared. The wash was
far longer than the old caravan lore suggested, and the springs marked on the
guides' memory had either moved or never existed. The column spent four days
crossing it on short rations of water, and two of the pack animals were lost —
not crew, but animals, a distinction the quartermaster's ledger is careful to
preserve. Korrin ordered the daily survey pace slowed so that the foraging
parties could range wider, and the expedition's reserve of grain was opened
earlier than planned.

## The Plateau Interior

Beyond the Bonewalk the plateau opened into a country of low stone ridges and
shallow basins, each basin holding a seasonal lake that the surveyors had to
sound and chart. This was the heart of the work. For sixty days the column
moved in a slow zigzag, never more than fifteen miles between camps, while the
two survey parties leapfrogged ahead and behind. The clerks' ledger from this
period is the most detailed part of the entire record: it names ninety-one
landmarks, fixes the position of nineteen reliable springs, and notes the
forage value of every basin.

The interior was also where the expedition's discipline was tested. The
foraging was thinner than expected, the nights were bitterly cold for the
season, and a low fever moved through the camp in the seventh week. The medics
contained it, and the ledger records that no one died of the fever, though it
laid up nearly a third of the column for several days each. Korrin used the
enforced halt to have the clerks make fair copies of the survey work to date,
reasoning that if the rough ledgers were lost the fair copies might survive.
This decision was later praised at the inquiry as the single act that
preserved the expedition's value.

## Method of the Survey

It is worth describing how the survey itself was actually done, because the
accuracy of the Korrin map was the expedition's whole reason for being. The
surveyors worked with the great theodolite for angle, the chain reels for
distance, and the brass levels for height. At each camp the lead party would
establish a station, sight back to the previous station and forward to a
chosen landmark, and read the chain distance along the traveled track. The
clerks transcribed the readings the same evening, and Korrin checked the
arithmetic himself before the fair copies were made.

The plateau was a difficult country to survey precisely because it was so
featureless. A basin looked much like the basin before it, and a stone ridge
gave few sharp landmarks to sight on. The surveyors compensated by building
small cairns at every station, so that the return leg could resight the same
points and close the survey loop. This cairn-building slowed the column but
gave the map a self-checking quality that later cartographers admired: any
error in the outbound survey would reveal itself when the return leg failed to
close on the same cairn.

The clerks' ledger records that the survey closed to within a tolerable margin
at all but three stations, and that the three exceptions were all in the
interior basins where the fever had disrupted the work. Korrin had those three
basins resurveyed on the return, which added four days to the journey but
removed the only serious doubts from the final map.

## The Salt Flats

The column reached the edge of the salt flats — the expedition's farthest
objective — on the hundred and second day. The flats themselves could not be
crossed; they were never meant to be. The surveyors fixed the position of the
flats' western margin, sounded the two brine springs at its edge, and raised a
cairn that later travelers used as a fixed reference point for decades. Korrin
declared the outbound objective met and ordered the return.

The return was always going to be the hard part. The provisions calculated for
a hundred and forty days were now stretched, the animals were worn, and the
season was turning. Korrin chose a more direct return route along the southern
ridges, gambling that the shorter distance would outweigh the unknown ground.
For three weeks the gamble paid off. The southern ridges held more game than
the interior basins, and the foraging parties kept the column fed.

## The Greywind

The trouble that defined the expedition's reputation came on the return, in
the high saddle the guides named the Greywind for the cold gale that funneled
through it. The column entered the saddle in deteriorating weather and was
caught by a three-day storm of sleet and wind far more violent than the season
should have allowed. The wagons could not move; the animals could not be kept
warm; the survey instruments had to be abandoned in a cached pit and recovered
by a later party two years afterward.

It was in the Greywind that the expedition suffered its only losses of crew.
The storm and the cold killed some outright and scattered others who were
never found; the medics' final tally, read into the record at the closing
inquiry and never afterward disputed, was that six crew members were lost in
the Greywind saddle. The quartermaster's roll names each of them: two porters,
one hunter, one drover, one of the younger surveyors, and a guide. Marshal
Korrin himself survived, though the inquiry noted that he never again led a
field expedition and spent his remaining years editing the plateau map in the
Cartographers' Hall.

## Aftermath

The battered column descended the Hallsreach stair late in the autumn, far
behind schedule and short of provisions, but with the fair copies of the
survey intact. The Cartographers' Hall judged the expedition a success in
every sense that mattered to a mapmaker: the plateau map produced from the
Korrin ledgers was accurate enough to guide caravans for the next thirty
years, and many of the names the clerks recorded — the Twin Cisterns, the
Bonewalk, the Greywind — are still in use today.

The closing inquiry, convened at Hallsreach the following winter, examined the
expedition's conduct in detail. It commended Korrin's decision to make fair
copies of the survey in the interior, criticized the optimistic provisioning,
and concluded that the southern return route, while it had cost the expedition
in the Greywind, had probably saved it overall by shortening the time on the
plateau. The inquiry's final summary observed that an expedition that returns
its map and the great majority of its people from country that thorough is, by
the standards of the frontier, a fortunate one.

## The Closing Inquiry in Detail

The inquiry sat for nineteen days and heard testimony from every surviving
member of the expedition. Its proceedings, bound in two volumes, are the
fullest source for the venture's later stages, because by the Greywind the
clerks' daily ledger had become fragmentary and the fair copies had been
sealed for protection rather than kept current.

The inquiry's first task was to confirm the roll. The depot warden's stamped
gate-count from the morning of the muster was read against Vant's
quartermaster roll and the medics' final tally, and the three documents were
found to agree. This agreement is why the expedition's figures are quoted with
such confidence today: the count of those who set out and the count of those
lost were each recorded by more than one independent hand and checked against
the others at the inquiry.

The inquiry's second task was to apportion judgment. It commended Vant's
insistence on logging counts at every gate and ford, which it called the
practice that made an honest reckoning possible. It commended Korrin's order
to make fair copies in the interior. It criticized the provisioning plan, as
already noted, and it examined at length the decision to take the southern
return route through the Greywind. After hearing the surveyors and the
surviving guide, the inquiry concluded that the southern route, for all that
it had cost the column in the saddle, had most likely shortened the time on
the plateau enough to save the expedition as a whole from a worse fate on the
longer northern track.

The inquiry's final summary, much quoted afterward, observed that an
expedition that returns its map and the great majority of its people from
country that thorough is, by the standards of the frontier, a fortunate one.
Korrin accepted the summary without objection and asked only that the names of
the six lost crew be entered in full in the bound proceedings, which was done.

For later travelers the most useful legacy was the chain of fixed reference
points — the salt-flat cairn, the nineteen charted springs, the Greywind cache
pit — that turned a featureless plateau into navigable country. The Korrin
ledgers themselves are preserved in the Cartographers' Hall, and this account
is the abstract drawn from them for readers who want the shape of the story
without the daily detail.
